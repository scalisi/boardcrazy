Board Crazy Reviews Mare Nostrum Empires

This week Board Crazy reviews Mare Nostrum: Empires, a board game designed by Serge Laget and published by Academy Games. In Mare Nostrum: Empires, you control one of five empires bordering the Mediterranean Sea. Check out our reviews of Mare Nostrum: Empires below.

D reviews Mare Nostrum Empires

You know, it’s way easier to pad the length of a review when there are obvious things you dislike about your subject. I’m sitting here trying to gather my thoughts on Mare Nostrum: Empires and not getting much beyond “boy that game was pretty fun, I’d sure like to play it again.” So… let’s start with some sexy, sexy background information. Empires is a crowdfunded revamping of the 2003 release Mare Nostrum, both of which are designed by Serge Laget. The update doesn’t make drastic changes to the original game, but it does make a number of smaller ones that make a significant difference. The original Mare Nostrum was not a bad game, but rather a promising-yet-flawed one. You pretty much had to play with a full five players to get a worthwhile experience, and the legitimate strategy options were still limited for each empire. Fortunately, this new version has gone a long way towards amending these issues.

The Pyramid Wonder Card – one of the four victory conditions. You need either 12 coins or 12 different resources to buy it.

We’ve played the game a couple of times now with the three-player setup, and I’m pleased to say that it is a totally acceptable way to play the game. The game is still best with the full complement, but you needn’t worry if you can’t wrangle up five people together to play it. Also, it feels like a wider variety of strategies are viable for each empire. The setup is always the same, so there can only be so much variety, but the Romans don’t have to play with a heavy military focus, for example. A big reason for this is the inclusion of new ways to win the game. In the original, winning was accomplished either by collecting four heroes/wonders or building the pyramids. Those are still included, but now the game can be won by holding four major cities or leading all three of the political tracks, giving players more things to consider when developing their own strategies. These additions are good, although in my experience the original ways to win are probably still the easiest to accomplish, which is a minor disappointment because they’re the least interesting.

Every type of resource you can collect in Mare Nostrum: Empires

I have some minor quibbles with Mare Nostrum: Empires, but nothing major. The game now uses tokens to represent the resources instead of cards, and if there is a change to the remake that I think is for the worse, this is it. The tokens might make them feel more like resources, I guess, but they’re a hassle to organize and they’re constantly being passed back and forth. Additionally – and I may start sounding like a broken record soon – I will never be a fan of using dice rolls to settle combat conflicts. To the game’s credit, it does a good job of mitigating the randomness of the dice by using custom ones which have a smaller variety of pips. Nonetheless, it’s still not an optimal system, especially when you’re dealing with legendary military strategists like Hannibal.

For the most part, though, I really enjoy Mare Nostrum: Empires. I don’t love it, and I’m pretty sure I never will (there are simply too many games that do similar things slightly better), but I will gladly keep playing it. It’s not a short game, but compared to its competitors it isn’t bad, largely thanks to its surprisingly simple rules. There is a lot to take in when you first read the rulebook, but there is little of the minutiae you’ll typically find in strategy games like this. The board is also quite nice and all of the components are solid, although I would recommend getting the Buildings Pack, which offers 3D figures to use in place of building tokens (if it’s within your budget). Overall, Mare Nostrum: Empires is a big improvement over the original and a fine, new strategy game that I’m pleased to have in our collection.

D’s Rating: Four Stars out of Five.

Will reviews Mare Nostrum Empires

Before I begin, I need to note that I’ve only played the 3-player variant of Mare Nostrum: Empires, which is quite different from the 4 or 5-player variants. In other words, this review is strictly about the 3-player version. With that being said, I quite liked many elements of Mare Nostrum. The gameplay is thought-out and fair, and the artwork is stunning and authentic-feeling. Sadly, the flaws of this game are very obvious when playing with only three people, and this fact kept me from truly loving a game that I fully expected to adore.

All of the game pieces for Carthage.

Starting with the good, I couldn’t help but to note how striking this game looks. After all, appearance is the first thing you notice about a board game (or anything really). The board itself is simple, with muted blues and browns dominating the proceedings, but once you add the colorful pieces to it, the contrast is visually sharp and sumptuous. As someone who considers himself a history buff, the map and other components (including the hero/wonders tokens) are very appealing to me, as they really capture the feel of the ancient Mediterranean region and cultures. While the artwork and pieces are definitely positives, the build quality definitely isn’t. The unit pieces are made with cheap feeling plastic, and the player sheets felt overly flimsy and bendable. The board and coins are robust enough though, so that’s good. Still, for every visual positive, there’s a physical negative.

Gameplay-wise, the first few games of Mare Nostrum are really exciting. Rounds are composed of a basic structure: you gather resources, build units and structures, move and attack, and then consolidate power at the end. You do this until someone achieves one of the several wins conditions, which include building a pyramid or controlling five heroes / wonders. In a 3-player game, players can choose from three civilizations: Rome, Greece, and Carthage. Rome is a military power, Greece is a cultural juggernaut, and Carthage is a resource machine. Again, all of this feels very historically accurate, which adds to the excitement when their empires start to grow and conflicts become imminent. If recreating historical wars was the goal of the game designers, then they did a fantastic job. Mare Nostrum often ends up simulating the past, and I loved every minute of it.

The starting position for Carthage, Rome, and Greece (in order from top to bottom)

However, just like my complaint with the art versus the build quality, for every positive, there’s a negative. This also applies to the gameplay, which quickly becomes predictable and stale the more you play. In a 3-player game, the board is smaller because certain regions are cut off, which means there are only so many places to go. Similarly, there are fewer resources available, which means players will end up battling over the same materials. Certain scenarios end up playing out the same way almost every time. For instance, Greece almost always conquers Troy before worrying about anything else, while Rome moves on Syracuse quickly and decisively. After all, Carthage always starts off weaker on the unit front, so they can’t effectively counter for a few rounds. Whoever plays as Carthage will actually get the most choice in Mare Nostrum, as they can either attack Greece or Rome (if those countries decide to attack each other, and they probably will). Yes, there are the games where Rome attacks Greece and Carthage sails north to invade Rome. And yes, imitating history is thrilling and appeases the inner history nerd in some of us. Unfortunately, the more you do this, the less you care. The replayability is just not there, and it quickly becomes apparent that this game was designed for 4-5 players, with three being more of a bonus option.

If you’re looking for a game to have fun with while playing with your friends, I recommend you look elsewhere. It’s hard to talk when playing Mare Nostrum: Empires – there’s a lot to think about, even if you know what everyone else is going to do. It’s also time consuming, so budget for at least 2-3 hours of gameplay. I consider both of these things to be negatives because I like board games that encourage interaction and brevity. On the other hand, if you’re in the mood for a historical strategy game and have several hours to kill, you’ll probably really enjoy Mare Nostrum (at least the first couple playthroughs). All in all, I really appreciate the positive aspects of this game, but almost every one is met with or overshadowed by a negative counterpart. And when you consider the time and effort that is put into Mare Nostrum, I’m just not sure it’s worth it.

I give Mare Nostrum a: C

Graham reviews Mare Nostrum Empires

I feel it’s almost not fair for me to review a game like Mare Nostrum: Empires after only two plays. In spite of that, I will still share some initial thoughts I had after playing it.

The Mediterranean Sea was once known as “Mare Nostrum”, meaning “Our Sea”. In this game, you control one of five ancient empires that borders Mare Nostrum (Rome, Greece, Carthage, Egypt, or Babylon). As one of these empires, you fight for victory through trade, culture, and military might. Immediately, this leads me to one of my main issues with Mare Nostrum: Empires – the sea is not really a focal point of the game, other than that it borders these empires. You get to build Triremes and use them as warships or as transports for your Legions, but the ships are not really a necessity for victory. It would have been awesome if there was a victory condition involving the Mediterranean Sea, or if there were resources that you could collect from the sea. With the current version of the game, it’s possible for you to win without ever buying a ship. It almost feels like the name was chosen because it sounds cool (which it totally does).

An example of the trade tokens. This token is chosen by the trade leader and determines how many resources are traded in the round.

With that said, there are a lot of things a really enjoyed about Mare Nostrum: Empires. The first thing that comes to mind is the trading mechanic. Initially, it seems somewhat bland, but eventually you’ll realize you have to be very clever and careful with the goods that you give and take because you’re required to give up any resources you don’t spend on your turn. A lot of the items you purchase (especially in the beginning), are sold in multiples of three, so you need to plan accordingly to make sure you are not wasting any resources. Another thing I really enjoyed about Mare Nostrum: Empires is the leader track – I really love this mechanic. It reminds me of the King’s Court in A Game of Thrones: The Board Game, which is one of my favorite games. My only gripe about the leader track is that I wish there was power given to not only to the player in 1st position, but also to the player in 2nd position (for a 3 player game). For example, it would be nice if the person in the 2nd position on the trade track had more influence than the person in the 3rd position and so on. How do you accomplish that? I’m not totally sure, but it would be beneficial because during our playthrough of Mare Nostrum, I gave up on some leader tracks because there was just no chance of me ever reaching 1st position, regardless of the player in 2nd position.

One last thing I would like to mention, is I really appreciate the way battles are handled in this game. Recently, it seems board games are pushing away from dice rolling as a mechanic for determining battle outcomes. However, if it’s handled correctly, dice rolling is my favorite way for battles to be fought. It provides suspense for something that should be suspenseful.

Overall, I really enjoyed my time playing Mare Nostrum: Empires. I thought the game succeeded in a lot of the game mechanics and I can’t wait to play it again (with hopefully 4 or 5 players). I give Mare Nostrum: Empires one thumb up.
