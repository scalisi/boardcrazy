Board Crazy Reviews Tortuga 1667

This week, Board Crazy reviews Tortuga 1667, a tabletop game designed by Travis and Holly Hancock and published by Facade Games. Tortuga 1667 is a game in which you play as a pirate trying to secure all of the gold for yourself. Keep reading to see what we thought of the Tortuga 1667.

D reviews Tortuga 1667

(Author’s note: this review is meant to accompany our gameplay video and will not go in-depth on the game’s rules. If you’re interested in learning how the game is played, please watch the video. It’s not bad.)

So, this is different. I must admit that I’m not entirely comfortable writing this review. The playthrough in our video is the only time I’ve played Tortuga 1667. Every other game I’ve reviewed thus far I’ve played on at least two occasions. Furthermore, we didn’t exactly play with what I assume is an ideal number of players, given the limitations of our setup. I’ll give my honest thoughts here, but take my score with a grain of salt. I imagine we might even revise these reviews in the future once we’ve played it again with larger groups.

Since we did a super fancy unboxing this time around, I guess I’ll start with the components. As I mentioned in the video, there is a lovely dollop of quality which perseveres throughout this package. The box, fashioned to look like an old book, is both cool to look at and nicely functional. There’s ample room inside for all the pieces and the map, and the magnetic lid keeps everything in place. Speaking of the map, the decision to make it a piece of fabric, evoking the spirit of old treasure maps, is really neat. It’s not cheap, though, clearly made out of a sturdy material that should last well. The art is all pretty swell too, with some amusing caricatures of the real-life pirates whom the players control. The inclusion of the histories of the pirates, boats, and titular island in the back of the rulebook is another really nice add-on.

Tortuga 1667 is also very well-designed with regards to its rules. The game is very easy to understand in terms of what you can do on your turn and what you must do to win. The backs of all the character cards also offer helpful reminders of all the actions you may perform. While straightforward, the rulebook does an excellent job clarifying any questions that may arise. There wasn’t a single situation in our game where we remained uncertain if we were playing it correctly. Rules clarity is something that I’ve found distressingly rare since we started this website, so this is a major point in the game’s favor.

The Game Mat

Most importantly, I think Tortuga 1667 is a fun game. With three players, there isn’t much mystery in where everyone’s loyalties lie, but that doesn’t make the maneuvering and scheming unsatisfying. There was genuine tension at the end of our game, as all of us were in contention to win. The turns are also very quick, which leads to minimal downtime. Obviously, there would be more downtime with more players, but I imagine that would be offset by the additional fun of figuring out your allies and enemies throughout the game.

I’m going to give Tortuga 1667 high marks. It’s well-made and well-designed, so I feel confident that this is definitely a good game, at least with three players. It might be a great game though, and I can’t wait to find that out. Maybe if our studio and budget grows, we can revisit this one with a bigger cast in the future. But, for now, it’s going to get a very solid four stars, with an asterisk.

D’s Rating: Four Stars* out of Five.

Will reviews Tortuga 1667

I need to start paying more attention to games on Kickstarter. Wait, let me rephrase that – I need to start paying for games on Kickstarter – there we go. Even though we write a monthly crowdfunding spotlight article, I’ve only invested a handful of times, but something tells me that’s going to change. Any skepticism I felt toward crowdfunding was assuaged after playing our tabletop game for this week, Tortuga 1667. This pirate-themed experience is published by Façade Games and is worth every penny that my colleague Graham spent when backing it on Kickstarter. It’s concise, intelligent, balanced, and most importantly, fun. And when you consider it was designed for up to nine players but worked perfectly fine with just three, you’ll realize it’s actually pretty special.

The Spanish Armada Card – when this event card is played, the game ends.

Immediately, I was struck by Tortuga 1667’s box, which appears to be an old, leather bound book until you get your hands on it. The cover is rather plain, but once you open it up and see all of the components neatly tucked into their spots, you realize how awesome this box truly is. Everything fits flawlessly, from the game mat to the cards, and there’s even a hidden pair of magnets that ensure the box seals firmly every time. The designers really went the extra mile to make Tortuga 1667’s container simultaneously innovative but old-looking. Add on the remarkable artwork featured on the cards, and you really have an aesthetically immersive experience.

Gameplay-wise, this game isn’t too shabby as well. The goal of Tortuga 1667 is to move treasure to areas you control, whether it’s on one of the ships or on Tortuga. For more details on the mechanics, I recommend watching our video or the tutorial video that Façade Games put on YouTube. Most of the gameplay is done through the drawing of Event Cards that can be beneficial or detrimental depending on your strategy and luck. Drawing a Black Spot and ending up marooned on Tortuga almost always stinks, but drawing a Pistol and marooning someone else is almost always glorious, so it all balances out in the end. And those are just two of the cards; there’s a satisfying amount of Events that will keep you on your toes.

In addition, there’s also a well thought-out mechanic regarding Vote Cards. Let’s say you’re the first mate and have ambitions for the captaincy – well, you can call for a mutiny. In that case, all of the players on that ship play Vote Cards secretly to determine if the mutiny is successful. Since the entire crew is involved, this process doesn’t feel arbitrary; it actually feels like a real life mutiny. Players also utilize their Vote Cards during the Attack phase. This is interesting because certain players on a ship may want the attack to fail in order to make certain the treasure total stays in their favor. And since Vote Cards are always shuffled before they’re revealed, the players’ loyalties remain a secret. I found the voting in Tortuga 1667 to be intense and annoying, but in a good way. Things don’t always go your way, and this game likes to remind you of that fact. So when a vote finally falls in line with your strategy, it truly feels amazing. The voting is just another element of the gameplay that’s really satisfying.

A Vote Card –  The top section is used to vote on attacks, the middle section is used to vote on brawls, and the bottom section is used to vote on mutinies.

Now, I will acknowledge that it’s easier to spot the positives than the negatives when you’ve only played something a few times. Sniffing out a game’s flaws sometimes takes multiple sessions with varying groups of players. With Tortuga 1667, I’m not quite sure how well the game works with larger groups since I’ve only played with smaller ones. That being said, I can state that a playthrough of Tortuga 1667 with merely two or three players is less than ideal. A lot of the game’s mechanics are better suited for bigger parties, which means that certain elements of the gameplay are all but absent with little groups. In other words, the fewer the players, the less dynamic Tortuga 1667 becomes. This is a real shame, and I can’t help but to think there are solutions to this problem that more play-testing could’ve revealed. I don’t know – maybe I’m wrong. Either way, hopefully the designers release an update or expansion that fixes this one-day.

So basically, Tortuga 1667 is a slightly flawed but mostly terrific tabletop game. The box and artwork are up there with the best I’ve seen in the last year, and the gameplay is truly enjoyable. Hell, I didn’t even get into the fact that it’s a pirate game, and pirates are always frickin’ cool. It may be tough to get your hands on a copy as of now, but once Tortuga 1667 hits retail shelves, I recommend checking it out. It’s well worth the time and the investment.

I give Tortuga 1667 a: A-

Graham reviews Tortuga 1667

Considering that this was my first experience backing a Kickstarter game and that our first playthrough was the video we did on the channel, my review of Tortuga 1667 might be slightly different than my normal reviews, and I might be slightly biased. Also, I should note that this game can be played with up to nine players and is probably meant to be played with more than three players.

Treasure Chests – Each player is trying to control the most treasure chests.

With all of that said, the first thing I want to mention is the quality of the components. Tortuga 1667 comes with a very nice game map that looks like a treasure map and feels like a mouse pad. It’s a very nice material that doesn’t slide around too easily, it feels durable, and it rolls up really nicely for clean-up. The cards all feel like standard playing cards, with some eye-catching artwork, and they are also very easy to understand. The wooden pieces used for the meeples, treasure chests, and the rowboat explosion are on par with most games. There is a nice burlap sack that’s used for the random player placement at the beginning of the game. It’s probably not a necessary component, but it feels like a high quality bag for a board game. Tortuga 1667 cost me a total of $27 ($23 for the game and $4 for shipping), which makes the quality of the pieces even more impressive. You really get great bang for your buck when looking at just the quality of the game components.

Game map close-up, with a rowboat explosion!

The quality of the game pieces is an important part of the tabletop game experience, but as I mentioned in our video, all that’s really needed to play a game is the rulebook and some very basic pieces that can really be made out of anything. The mechanics and theme are really the most important parts of a game. This is where I think Tortuga 1667 really stands out. The pirate theme is a really fun and exciting theme that has, in my opinion, not been overdone, and I love that you get to play as a pirate that actually existed. After you read the rulebook for the first time, the game seems a little overwhelming, but as soon as you start playing, it becomes very simple and enjoyable. You have a clear goal (to end with more treasure than the other person, unless you’re the Dutch) and a wide variety of actions that you can take. The actions include: peaking at Event Cards, playing an Event Card, forcing someone else to play an Event Card, attacking, marooning another player, moving yourself to or from the island, leading a mutiny, moving treasure around, and starting a brawl. As you can see, there’s a lot you can do, and Travis & Holly did a great job balancing the actions. You are only allowed to take certain actions depending on where you are located on a ship. For example, a Captain can call for an attack or maroon any of his crewmen, but only the Cabin Boy can move treasure from one side to the other. So, it’s not always a bad thing to be the Cabin Boy, and it’s not always beneficial to be the Captain.

I have a few concerns about the game. First, Tortuga 1667 feels like it’s all about player reactions. What I mean by that is, it seems there isn’t much long term strategy that goes into the game. I wouldn’t be surprised if most games come down to a lucky card at the end. The second thing is, there doesn’t seem to be too much benefit in being deceptive. Deception is definitely an important part of the game, but I was very clear about my loyalty from the beginning, and I don’t think it hurt me at all. However, this might just be specific to games with fewer players. I imagine that as more people get involved, it becomes a lot tougher figure out who’s who. It seems as if it was put in place so that there’s uncertainty in loyalty; not so that you can try to use that uncertainty to trick people, if that makes sense.

Overall, Tortuga 1667 is a very well-designed game that’s nicely crafted. I’m very interested to play a larger game and see how it plays out in regards to deception. I cannot give Travis and Holly Hancock enough credit. Tortuga 1667 would be an impressive game for an established designer to create, let alone someone’s second attempt. I give Tortuga 1667 two thumbs up.
