Board Crazy Reviews Covert

This week Board Crazy reviews Covert, a board game designed by Kane Klenko and published by Renegade Game Studios. It’s Cold War Europe and you need to get a tactical advantage on your enemy through the use of espionage. Keep reading below to see what Board Crazy thinks of this unique dice placement game!

D reviews Covert

(Author’s note: this review is meant to accompany our gameplay video and will not go in-depth on the game’s rules. If you’re interested in learning how the game is played, please watch the video. It’s not bad.)

Covert is a bit of a mess. It’s a shame because there is a good game tucked away in its box, but some curious design decisions sour what should be a sweet experience. It has a cool theme, where the players are pitted against each other as rival spies zipping around Europe during the Cold War. But then it lets that premise down by not really allowing for much conflict between the players or intrigue on the board. There are brief inconveniences on occasion, but for the most part, everyone is playing their own game in a race to the finish. And parts of that game are fun! Rolling dice, coming up with a strategy, and seeing it through can be quite satisfying in this game. Of course, in order to come up with a strategy, it helps to have a firm grasp on the rules, and with this game that’s easier said than done.

Players can use the items on the agency cards to match the items on the mission cards.

See what I mean? Covert is such a mish-mash of ideas and rules that I’m struggling to even focus on one element of it for more than a sentence. Allow me to regain my composure before I continue…

Okay, so let’s focus on the good stuff first. I think everything involving the dice is pretty well-done. Choosing what to do and in which order to do it is fun and strategic without being overwhelming. The board is well-designed, with clear-cut spaces for each of the game’s facets, and the map is nice to look at. The rules for the various actions you can take are also written out under each action, which is nice. However, there is at least one instance where the text is not identical to what is printed in the rulebook. The components are mostly fine, although some of the “intel” tokens have specks of the wrong color of paint on them. There are also some odd choices in the art design. Despite being set in the Cold War, some of the agents look straight out of modern times, and the credit card depicted in the game has a chip on it, which is wildly anachronistic.

Alright, apparently I can’t be strictly positive about Covert. I wanted to like this game because I like a lot of its ideas; I just don’t think they all come together coherently. A lot of this is the fault of the rulebook, which goes to great lengths to highlight things that are obvious, while being vague or inconsistent in enough ways to make sure that you’re never completely certain that you’re playing the game correctly. So yeah, it’s going to be a shorter-than-usual review this week. I’ll keep an eye out for a revised edition, but for now I’d say skip this one unless you’re absolutely obsessed with dice placement games.

D’s Rating: Two Stars out of Five.

Will reviews Covert

I love spy films, and my taste for them isn’t as simple as the action-packed kind à la James Bond or Jason Bourne. No, I like them all, even the slower-paced, more realistic entries like Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy and The Lives of Others. That’s the main reason I was pretty excited to try out Kane Klenko’s Covert, a board game published by Renegade Game Studios. I didn’t expect something that was action packed, like Bond; I expected a high stakes but subdued Cold War spy experience, so think more Bridge of Spies. I had it in my head that Covert would be full of secret missions, double agents, and all the mischief that comes with the genre. While some of those things are present in this game, it really doesn’t live up to its theme.

Each character has a special ability they can use throughout the game.

One of the first things I always look for in a board game is how closely the artwork and pieces match up to the game’s theme and my expectations. Unfortunately, Covert was mostly a failure in this regard. Let’s start with the artwork – some of the playable spy characters and their tools (cameras, guns, etc.) appear out of place. Covert is set during the Cold War, so I expected older, clumsier looking devices, but some of them are newer and more anachronistic looking, which immediately threw me off. Not only this, the spy characters were drawn with a semi-modern flare – their dress and haircuts didn’t seem to mesh with the setting. On the other hand, the board itself did impress me with its simplistic design and eye for detail. Saint Petersburg is called Leningrad, for example. As I finally found something redeeming in the aesthetic design, I noticed that Stockholm is in the orange portion of the board, but its corresponding card is purple. Little mistakes like this should have been caught before Covert made it to print.

Since the artwork and piece design failed to live up to the theme, maybe the gameplay can make up for it, or so I thought. Again, I expected to go on missions and double-cross my opponents – you know, spy stuff. And while there are missions in Covert, they aren’t what you’d expect. Players aren’t tasked with any particular objectives, nor is their any juicy, exhilarating espionage. No, in the end your “missions” are to collect cards with specific icons on them, like the gun icon or the binoculars icon. Occasionally you’ll need to be in specific cities or regions as well, which does add a bit of color to the proceedings since you leave markers behind whenever you move from one city to another. If you collect enough of your opponents’ markers, then you can collect extra cards, which is actually a really thoughtful mechanic. Still, the missions are a huge letdown, and completing them feels more like work than fun. This is mainly because Covert barely feels like a spy experience most of the time.

Most of the games strategy revolves around the different actions.

In fact, the best parts of the game are those that involve strategic thought and some mercilessness. To put it simply, every player has to roll dice that they will eventually use to indicate their upcoming moves. On the top of Covert’s board, there are six spots on each of the action sections, which include “draw an agent card”, “draw a mission card”, and so on. If you want to take such an action, you’ll either need to be the first to play a die on that section, or you’ll need to play a die that is adjacent to an already played die. So if your opponent has placed a die numbered 4, for example, then you’ll need to play a three or a five. Here’s the kicker: what numbers your opponents rolled is public knowledge, so if you play your dice right, you can properly screw people over. Preventing another player from making the move they want feels like something a spy would do – you’re ostensibly running interference. Finally, an element of the gameplay that feels thematically appropriate.

An example of a code card and the item you can collect from it.

Before I end, I should mention that the code breaking, cipher portion of Covert is also enjoyable and fits with the Cold War theme. Basically, there are numbers on the side of the board that you’ll have corresponding cards for, and if you move the numbers around correctly, you can earn the item on your cards. The cipher phase also presents players with another opportunity to mess with their opponents’ strategies. If only the rest of Covert was as well thought-out as this… Oh well, sometimes board games look better on paper or in images than they actually are. I wouldn’t go as far as to call Covert a disaster – it just comes off like a dice rolling, hand management game that had a Cold War spy theme slapped onto it. To me, this all feels disingenuous because I expected a spy game, which is how it’s presented after all. There definitely are positive qualities to Covert, but I can’t help but to feel the negative ones spoiled the experience for me. I do respect one thing about this game more than anything else though – it reminded me to temper my expectations.

I give Covert a: C

Graham reviews Covert

One of my biggest pet peeves in life is when something that should be simple gets turned into something unnecessarily complex. That pretty much sums up my feelings for Covert. I’ll give you the gist of the game – in Covert, you play as a spy trying to complete missions. You complete missions by matching the items or cities on the mission cards with items that you’ve collected and cities that you’re located in. Also, you place dice on various actions which give you the opportunity to either move or collect items that you need.
 
The rulebook is the main driver behind the complexity and confusion in Covert. I’ve played the game four times now, and I’m still not 100% sure I’m playing it correctly. The rules are written in very complex ways that make you second guess yourself. For instance, one of the actions is taking an “Agency Card”. Agency cards provide you with the items that you need to match to mission cards, among other things. The rule for this action states:
“For each die on the Agency Card Action Circle, a player may take one face-up Agency Card. The player must have an Agent in a city matching the region where the card is. A player can use two dice at once to take any Agency card, either face-up or face-down…”
Does this mean that a player can use two dice and take the face-down pile of Agency cards, look through the pile, and take the card they want? Or does this mean that the player can choose to take any one for the face-up cards or any face-down card at random? The rulebook has several instances of these scenarios where the explanation isn’t exactly clear on what you are allowed to do, and it often confuses things even more. Another thing that bothers me about Covert is that I really don’t see much cohesion among the elements of the game. The mechanics aren’t really tied together by the theme as much as they should be. The only mechanic that feels like you’re actually a spy is the code-breaking, which I’ll admit is a lot of fun. Other than that, these elements could have been wrapped around any theme and I wouldn’t notice a difference.

The Decoder – move the decoder tiles around to match the code shown on your code card. If you successfully match the code, you get either two victory points (at the end of the game) or the item shown on the back of the card.

Finally, the game doesn’t really allow for much interaction among the players. This is the same criticism I had about Roll Player and Jump Drive. A great part of board gaming is the personal interaction between players, and it bums me out when that aspect is missing from a game. In Covert, you have your mission cards and make your actions – you don’t ever really have to worry about the other player. The exception to this is the code breaking mechanic, which is objectively the best part about the game.
Covert was disappointing because I really love the theme. I just mentioned in my review of Letters from Whitechapel how much I love deception, bluffing, and mystery, and they all seem like they would be a natural fit for this game. Unfortunately, Covert doesn’t really do it for me, and I would give it two thumbs down if it wasn’t for the awesome code breaking mechanic. As is, I give Covert one thumb down.
