Board Crazy Reviews Element

This week Board Crazy reviews Element, a board game designed by Mike Richie & Grant Wilson and published by Rather Dashing Games. Element is a grid-movement game, in which you try to capture your opponent by harnessing the power of the four elements – Wind, Fire, Earth, and Water. Read below to see what we thought of Element!

D reviews Element

(Author’s note: this review is meant to accompany our gameplay video and will not go in-depth on the game’s rules. If you’re interested in learning how the game is played, please watch the video. It’s not bad.)

This is the cloth bag from which the elements are drawn.

Element, published in 2017 by Rather Dashing Games, is an update and expansion to Mike Richie’s original 2008 release of the same name. The original release was a two-player game that featured dice rolling for the random selection of element stones, while this new version is for 2-4 players and has replaced the dice rolling with a cloth bag from which the stones are drawn. However, the goal of the game remains the same: try to capture your opponent using the element stones before you get captured yourself. These elements are the classical ones: fire, water, earth and wind, and they each have their own unique abilities that players use to block their opponent and move themselves around the board.

Element finds a nice balance between simplicity and complexity. The game is easy to understand, but is in no way mindless. The recommended age for Element is 14+, but I imagine children younger than 14 could easily enjoy this game, and there is certainly nothing objectionable about its content. The rulebook is laid out very nicely, with the general rules of the game taking up the first couple of pages, and the rest dedicated to explaining each element in detail, with several visual examples provided. It may not answer every question a player might have, but I think from context anyone should be able to play the game without issue after reading it over once.

The Earth Sage – You can call him… Tim.

The components of Element are a bit of a mixed bag. Grant Wilson, better known as a former host of Ghost Hunters (no, seriously), is the game’s artist, and I think he did a good job overall. The sage pieces in particular are really well done, looking and feeling like something old and handcrafted. The board itself is serviceable. It’s designed to look faded and weathered, and while I generally like it, I imagine some people with poor eyesight may have difficulty with some of the lines. The element stones are disappointing, unfortunately. They are made of plastic and while completely functional, they feel cheap and artificial, whereas the rest of the game does not. This was presumably done for cost reasons, but if Element is successful enough that a “deluxe” version ever comes out with better pieces, I would not hesitate to pick that up.

Overall, I quite like Element and recommend it. I specifically enjoy the game’s win condition, where you are only trying to capture the player to your right. I initially figured it would be a last man standing type of game, but having to stop one opponent while not completely ignoring the player trying to capture you leads to some fun strategic moments. I have some minor quibbles with the gameplay, specifically the completely random nature of the stone drawing. It keeps all the players on an even playing field, but I feel like some mechanic could have been implemented to give the players a bit more control. Fortunately, this game seems particularly house rule-able, for lack of a better term. I already have an idea regarding the (typically identical) sage pieces in order to give them a bit of individuality. Maybe we’ll try that in another video, but if you liked what you saw in this one, give Element a look.

D’s Rating: Three and One-Half Stars out of Five.

Will reviews Element

In the beginning, there were only the four elements: fire, water, earth, and wind. Now, imagine if there were sages who could control these elements and bend them to their will – that’s the basic idea behind Element, a board game designed by Mike Richie and published by Rather Dashing Games. Abstract strategy games like this don’t appeal to everyone, but there’s something strangely likable about Element, even with its flaws.

Gameplay-wise, the goal of the game is to surround the sage of the player to your right. If you do so successfully and they are unable to move, you win the game. In order to surround them, you’ll need to place element tokens on the board, and each token (fire, water, earth & wind) features a different special ability. For example, water tokens chain together to create rivers that move around the board, while earth tokens create mountain chains that are almost impossible to pass over. Of course, sage figures can move around on their turns, so placing the element tokens in the correct spots requires a good deal of foresight (and occasionally dumb luck).

The Fire Element

All of this means that Element is not the kind of board game that affords its players the luxury of a long-term strategy. After every turn, you’ll be forced to reconsider any plans you literally just made – there’s little time to relax. Whether or not this is negative to you will depend on the kind of tabletop gamer you are, but if I had to guess, some hardcore players will dislike the lack of control they have over the game. And what little control the game gives its players, such as picking tokens out of the elements bag, comes with a great deal of luck. For instance, if you’re playing and need a wind token that allows you to leapfrog out of a sticky situation, there’s no guarantee that a grey wind token will be among the ones you draw. Most players will understand that luck is part of the game and therefore should influence their strategy, but this also means that some sessions won’t be all that fun. If there’s a disproportionate amount of one kind of element on the board, it will negatively affect a game. This happened to us and was extremely frustrating.

The Fire Sage – Is it gettin’ hot in here or what?

Personally, I don’t love Element, but I do see the merit of abstract games now that I’ve played it a few times. I do need to mention that the instruction manual confused some of our group more than once, and that’s always annoying. And then there’s the difficulties the game faces in regards to strategy and luck, which can be quite detrimental. Thankfully, there isn’t that much to learn, so once you get a handle on the gameplay, it plays fairly quickly. Also, Element looks great and includes some excellent build quality, and the overall game experience is unique and fun enough to appeal to most people. Some day I will return to Element, but for now, I’m going to enjoy other board games that are a bit more comfortable (mentally) to play.

I give it a: B-

Graham reviews Element

The Wind Sage – this sage will blow you away!

To preface, I feel bad – I’m about to give Element a somewhat negative review even though I enjoyed my time playing it. There are just too many nagging problems associated with this game. First things first, the rulebook – I found Element’s rulebook confusing. There are a lot of examples of gameplay covered, but there were still scenarios that we immediately questioned and could not find a clear answer to. On top of that, the situations that were covered could have been displayed a little more intelligibly. Poorly written rules frustrate me to no end because it’s something that’s so important! My next issue with the game is that, at a glance, it seems like it should play really quickly, but it doesn’t. I acknowledge that my colleagues and I can drag a game on longer than necessary, and I am especially guilty of over-thinking my moves. Still, I felt that during our play of Element, it moved slowly even if we were making our decisions quickly. There were often a lot of pieces that needed to be moved around on the board or removed from the game. In other words, the game is too high maintenance for what it’s supposed to be.

Earth, Wind, and Fire… oh and Water

Unfortunately for Element, Santorini is fresh in my mind, which is a much more impressive grid-movement game than Element. This game wasn’t able to accomplish the depth, quickness, and simplicity that Santorini offers. Lastly, another thing that kind of bugs me about Element is the element tokens. I feel the tokens should have been made with a different material, maybe a wood or glass. The plastic material didn’t fit the theme and style of the rest of the game. Obviously, this is a relatively minor complaint, but I did notice it.

With all of that said, there were definitely some positives to Element. First, I really enjoyed the individual powers of the elements. They were balanced, well thought out, sensible, and unique. I also loved that these powers stack! I thoroughly enjoy stackable powers in any game, and Element did a really good job with this. For instance, you can stack wind elements, which can have you jumping across half the board! Also, I thought the game mechanics were pretty simple, and once you got the hang of it, it really allows you to focus on tactics and strategy. Finally, one thing I realized about this game is that the luck aspect of it didn’t bother me as much as it does in some other games. I had a few pretty poor draws from the element bag (three or four times I drew three wind tokens), and I was still able to use the elements on the board and in my hand to make the moves I needed.

Overall, Element isn’t a standout game and doesn’t compete too well with the rest of the games in its genre. The rulebook is weak, and it played a little too slowly. I would give this game a thumbs down if it weren’t for the awesome mechanics of the element powers, which is basically the main part of the game. Therefore, I will give Element one thumb up and one thumb down.
