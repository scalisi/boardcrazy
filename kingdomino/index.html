Kingdomino Review

In this Kingdomino review, Board Crazy examines the 2017 Spiel Des Jahres winner. Kingdomino was designed by Bruno Cathala and published by Blue Orange Games. In this game, players compete by building the most resourceful kingdom. Read below to see what we thought of Kingdomino, the award-winning, tile placement game.

D reviews Kingdomino

(Author’s note: this review is meant to accompany our gameplay video and will not go in-depth on the game’s rules. If you’re interested in learning how the game is played, please watch the video. It’s not bad.)

Bruno Cathala’s Kingdomino, as you may or may not know, recently won the 2017 Spiel des Jahres. The German “Game of the Year” award is generally agreed to be the most prestigious of its sort in the board game industry, but I’ve often wondered about the consequences of winning the award. Certainly it’s good for sales, and thus from a business perspective it’s only a good thing, but I wonder if it’s more of a double-edged sword when it comes to reputation. For starters, the “Game of the Year” designation is somewhat of a misnomer, as the games considered for this award are family games with simpler rules and shorter playtimes on average. In fact, in 2011 they created the separate Kennerspiel des Jahres award for more complex games that appeal to connoisseurs/enthusiasts. To give an example from this year, Terraforming Mars was nominated for the Kennerspiel award. And I don’t know about you, but when I think about the premier board games of the last year, Terraforming Mars comes to mind way before Kingdomino does. Other heralded games like Scythe and Great Western Trail were not even finalists (although admittedly, I am unfamiliar with the nomination process/time window, so they may not have been eligible). So, from my perspective as an outsider who likes to keep himself abreast of the board gaming zeitgeist, I am curious if winning this award is likely to cause the people who care the most – the enthusiasts and designers – to sort of turn their noses up at the winners. Maybe that’s a silly thought, but looking back at the winners over the years reveals a mixed back of enduring classics, unspectacular games, and largely forgotten titles. Although, maybe that’s just the nature of awards in general.

Approaching Kingdomino as a reviewer though, I admit that I naturally feel compelled to scrutinize it a bit more thoroughly than I would a similar game without the GOTY tag. So, I’m going to do my best to judge it strictly on its own merits. And you know what? It’s pretty good! For starters, the game is highly replayable. It moves very quickly, with minimal downtime, and can easily be finished in about half an hour. These traits help offset the high degree of randomness that is found in the game and the feelings of “unfairness” that can come with it. Had some back luck with the tiles this game? Just play it again! And while that randomness is prevalent, the game is not without strategy. The way initiative is handled is clever. Choosing whether to go for the more valuable tiles now or waiting to see if something even better comes out next is a solid risk/reward system that can change the outcome of the game.

The Game Box… Everything just fits so nicely.

Kingdomino’s packaging and components are fine, but nothing spectacular. The “dominoes” which give the game its name are glossy, cardboard tiles that seem well-made, although we’ve hardly had a chance to put many miles on them. Cyril Bouquet’s art on the tiles and the little cardboard castles you build your kingdoms around are cute, but that’s about it. The game’s art and theme are really just for flavor; the real selling point of this game is definitely the gameplay. In fact (and I always feel bad saying something like this), the game could easily be recreated via a simple home art project or probably even with traditional dominoes.

The only real, vague criticism I have of Kingdomino is something that comes down to personal preference. I don’t like it when a game has you use the box as part of the experience, and in Kingdomino, you store the tiles for future draws in the box. This is probably just me being weird, but there is something about it that just irks me a little bit. I think part of it is just the fact that an open board game box just isn’t very nice to look at compared to a well-designed component that could achieve the same thing. And I think another part is that it breaks any tiny bit of immersion I’m trying to build up in my mind. You probably don’t care about any of that, but this is my review and I’m definitely sticking my tongue out at you, dear reader.

Overall, Kingdomino is definitely a well-designed game that I would recommend, especially to people who prefer family games. I don’t like it as much as the previous Bruno Cathala game we played, Yamatai, nor would I put it in the same company as previous Spiel des Jahres winners like Codenames or Catan, but it’s definitely a nice, little game that I’m glad we have in our collection.

D’s Rating: Three and One-Half Stars out of Five.

Will reviews Kingdomino

I think Kingdomino is good – there, I said it. This is hardly a controversial statement though, given that the game won the 2017 Spiel des Jahres award (Game of the Year). Bruno Cathala, the designer, revamped the basics of Dominoes in order to make a lighthearted family game with cute and engaging artwork (from Cyril Bouquet). Considering its pleasant simplicity, it’s not difficult to comprehend why so many people have enjoyed Kingdomino. Personally, I appreciate the easy-to-learn gameplay and brief playtime. However, despite all of these positives, I couldn’t help but to feel that this game is somewhat unremarkable. After all, when something wins the biggest award of the year, there are certain expectations.

An example of a 5×5 grid in Kingdomino. This grid would score you 37 points!

To start the game, each player gets a castle around which they will be building their kingdom. From then on, the players will take turns selecting tiles (or dominoes) and placing those tiles in a 5×5 grid around their castle. Some of these tiles will have crown symbols on them, and these crowns are how players earn victory points. So if you have a crown on a forest environment, and you play further forest tiles adjacent to that crown, you’ll earn more victory points. That’s Kingdomino’s gameplay in a nutshell – it’s really that simple. One of the hardest parts of designing a tabletop game is creating gameplay that’s comprehensible, so I applaud Cathala for making a game that’s truly intuitive. But then again, it’s basically Dominoes with an interesting set of new rules and restrictions. Can a re-skinning of a classic game really be considered groundbreaking enough to win the Spiel des Jahres?

The least valuable and most valuable dominoes.

As satisfying as it is to place down a tile that you know will raise your score, it’s equally unsatisfying when your opponent does the same. Choosing when to help yourself or when to sabotage your opponents is one of the best parts of Kingdomino, but it’s also one of its most frustrating. Honestly, I don’t think the game did enough to encourage its players to block or disrupt their opponents’ plans. In any session, there are certainly opportunities to spoil someone else’s strategy, but acting on those opportunities will almost certainly damage your own plans. You’ll end up asking yourself – what’s the point of hurting someone else if it obstructs my own path to victory? This leads to me to my biggest complaint in regards to Kingdomino – player interaction is almost unnecessary. As it is, you don’t need to worry about anyone other than yourself. Sure, other players can inconvenience you by taking the tile you wanted, but your ultimate strategy always remains the same – you’re going to do the best you can with what you got. Perhaps this is only true in a 2 or 3-player game, but it’s still a problem.

In the end, that’s all I have to say about Kingdomino. There’s really not much to this game, which is both good and bad. Its uncomplicated design makes it effortless to learn, and its shorter playtime makes it highly replayable. On the other hand, I don’t think there’s enough there to warrant a Game of the Year victory. It reminds me of the Academy Awards – take 1995 for instance. Pulp Fiction, Quiz Show, and The Shawshank Redemption were all nominated for Best Picture, but Forrest Gump ended up winning. Forrest Gump is a decent film, but was it better than its competition? Probably not, but it was the safe choice. That’s what Kingdomino feels like to me – the safe choice. It’s good, but it’s also unremarkable, and that’s a bummer.

I give Kingdomino a: B-

Graham reviews Kingdomino

After playing Kingdomino, it’s obvious why it was at the very least nominated for the 2017 Spiel Des Jahres. It’s very well balanced, it’s quick and easy to learn, and it’s fun for all ages.

This game is ultimately a tile-placement game combined with dominoes. In Kingdomino, you build your kingdom by placing the dominoes/tiles in a 5×5 grid around your kingdom’s castle. Just like in dominoes, you can only place a tile that matches a tile on your board. Scoring is really simple: you count up matching squares that are adjacent to each other and multiply that number by the number of crowns in that area. So if you have 4 squares of water next to each other and there is one crown in that area, that is worth four points. Yo do this for each area of land in your kingdom, add them all up, and that will be what your kingdom is worth. The player with the most valuable kingdom wins.

Placing those two mountains next to each other would earn you 10 points. Those are incredibly powerful dominoes.

For me, the fun of Kingdomino comes from trying to build the largest single resource area possible. This is usually a pretty good strategy too. So if you can find a way to combine some mountain tiles with a few of the very powerful two and three crown squares, you’ll most likely come out on top. But that’s not the only thing I like about Kingdomino though – it’s also one of the most balanced games I have played. For instance, when selecting your next domino, you have to choose between value and initiative. If you want the best tile on the board, then you are going to be last during the selection phase next turn. This creates a scenario where you will alternate initiative quite a bit, which means everyone has a fair opportunity at getting valuable tiles. Another fantastic quality of Kingdomino is how easy it is to learn. Spiel des Jahres nominees are typically family-friendly games that are intuitive, and Kingdomino definitely fits the bill. We learned how to play Kingdomino in under 10 minutes. It’s incredibly accessible, and there isn’t much to question. On top of that, it plays really quickly and it’s easy to play two or three games of Kingdomino in one sitting. The game may suffer from some replayibility issues, but since it plays so quickly and is easy to teach, people will most likely use it as a nice introductory game into the hobby and as a result, continue to play it.

I think Kingdomino’s biggest issue is that it seems to be lacking a “wow” factor. There is nothing in it that really blows your mind. Not that every new board game or Spiel Des Jahres winner needs to do something mind-blowing, but overall, I feel like there is nothing there to make Kingdomino stand out from any other game. A lot of the things I liked about this game have been accomplished by other games and it makes this game feel like just another face in the crowd. Regardless, it’s still a fun experience, and I look forward to playing Queendomino when it comes out. I give Kingdomino one thumb up.
