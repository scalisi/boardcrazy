Board Crazy Reviews Roll Player

This week Board Crazy reviews Roll Player, a board game designed by Keith Matejka and published by Thunderworks Games. Roll Player allows you to re-create the excitement that comes from creating a new character in Dungeons & Dragons, but with a twist. Check out our reviews below to see what we thought of Roll Player!

D reviews Roll Player

(Author’s note: this review is meant to accompany our gameplay video and will not go in-depth on the game’s rules. If you’re interested in learning how the game is played, please watch the video. It’s not bad.)

In the intro to our video for Keith Matejka’s Roll Player, I mention that some of the most fun you can have in any RPG occurs before the game actually starts, when you create your character. Maybe that’s just me, but I believe that’s true for a lot of people. I run a game of Dungeons & Dragons for five people (including the two fools whose reviews will follow mine), and I remember everyone having a good time when I sat down with them and helped them build their first characters and roll up their stats. Being creative is satisfying, seeing a creation of yours quickly take form is satisfying, and rolling dice is satisfying. And for better or worse, I believe everything I just wrote holds true for Roll Player as well.

Class Card Example – At the end of the game, you get reputation stars for every class attribute score you match with your dice values.

Before I go any further, I feel compelled to point out that, despite its name, Roll Player is decidedly not a role-playing game. We gave it a half-hearted try in the video, but the game isn’t built for it, nor does encourage it, either. What it is, is a pleasantly digestible strategy game built around dice rolling, with elements from popular RPGs used as trappings. This is neither good nor bad, but it is useful information to know ahead of time in order to set your expectations accordingly.

As I sort of implied above, some of the most fun I have when I play Roll Player occurs before the game really even starts. There is a certain thrill for me when I’m randomly choosing my character’s class and seeing what backstory and alignment cards I’ve been dealt, and the game provides enough variety with those things that you shouldn’t see much repetition on repeat plays. However, despite knowing how the game is played, I always end up disappointed that there isn’t slightly more to do with your character and their traits. Those various cards mostly just provide you with goals to meet and have very little influence on the way you should play the game. The setup is also when you get to roll the most dice at once (in order to get some of your character’s starting stats), which in my book makes it the best dice roll in the game, for what it’s worth.

The Dragonkin character sheet – Whenever you place a die into an attribute box you get to use the ability next to that roll. For example, if place a dice in the dex position you can swap any two dice on your character sheet.

Now, I realize I’m criticizing Roll Player for not being exactly the game I want it to be rather than reviewing it for the game that it is, but I’m doing this because I actually really like it and think it could be even better. The gameplay flows quickly and is really easy to understand, but there are enough things to keep track of and enough tools at everyone’s disposal to keep things strategic and tense. It’s also well-balanced for a game so dependent on something as random as dice rolling. Sometimes you’ll just have rotten luck, like Graham did in the video, but for the most part, the game offers enough ways to succeed that everyone should be pretty close when the scores are tallied in the end. The game is also well-made. I’m particularly fond of the clever way the character sheets are designed to incorporate your cards and dice. If Roll Player managed to include some role-playing elements with all of this goodness, I would probably love it, but as it is, Roll Player is still a fine game.

D’s Rating: Three and One-Half Stars out of Five.

Will reviews Roll Player

Roll Player is one of those games that frustrates you, but not for the reasons you’d probably think. It’s not imbalanced, nor is it shoddily made – it’s nothing like that. In fact, Roll Player is a clever game that’s very satisfying to play, for the most part. No, the reason it’s so frustrating is that it’s such an obvious concept for a board game, or at least, it is for anyone who’s a fan of Dungeons & Dragons. Who doesn’t love rolling a new character for an epic campaign? I mean, how did it take this long for someone to dream this up? Gah, I’m just annoyed that it wasn’t me!

Is neurosis really an evil trait to have?

Putting those frustrations aside, Roll Player deserves some real praise. After all, being such a good idea is definitely not a knock on the game; it’s a compliment. However, a solid concept is not enough; there needs to be follow-through. Gratefully, the designer (Keith Matejka) has pretty much nailed the ideal character-creation board game. The concept is simple: you roll dice, you choose dice, and you use them to create the best possible character based on the class and background cards you select at the beginning of the game. Yes, there’s a bit more to it than that, but the goal of the game is relatively straightforward – the player with the best character wins (usually).

Chain Leggings armor card that you can buy from the market.

Again, there is more to it. For instance, there’s an entire system based on collecting gold in order to purchase weapon, armor, skill, and trait cards from the “market”. These cards serve to aid your character in achieving the maximum number of reputation points (stars), the victory points of Roll Player. The skill and trait cards are the most interesting, as they involve moving around your alignment. If you want to earn some bonus points by being lawful good at the end of the game, then you’ll need to be careful when purchasing and utilizing cards that lean toward the side of evil (so maybe don’t be a pickpocket). Personally, I found the trait cards to be extremely important because they provide struggling players with an opportunity to get back into the game. For example, if you’re ending up with the crappy rolls (like 1’s and 2’s), you may be presented with an opportunity to purchase a trait card that rewards you with reputation for having a low intelligence or wisdom score. All of a sudden, being an idiot becomes a viable path toward victory, which is awesome. Most impressively, the cards are very well balanced; so much so, they may be some of the fairest cards I’ve ever seen in a board game. That’s some stellar game design – bravo.

Unfortunately, Roll Player is definitely not perfect. There are elements of luck involved, especially when rerolling dice while utilizing the intelligence ability. Sadly, this does mean that some unlucky players might not get rewarded with a victory, even if they’re the best or most experienced player at the table. Also, since every point matters in the end, meticulous players will take a while to min/max everything and make their decisions. Lastly, the art design left me wanting – it’s rather bland and uninspired, which is always disappointing. But there are multi-sided player sheets (for male and female), and there are tons of pretty dice, so yay!

In the end, I truly enjoy Roll Player, a game I find to be fair, thought-out, and engaging to play. All of our group’s sessions ended in close scores, which is a sign of this game’s balance. On the other hand, it’s not flawless – there are some issues with luck and dull art design. And to get a bit existential, you could ask: what’s the point of Roll Player? After all, you’re just creating a player, not using it. But that’s what’s so great about tabletop RPGs – you could use the character you make as your PC in a D&D campaign. Or instead, maybe you use it as a super powerful NPC; the sky’s the limit. All in all, one of the greatest compliments I can pay Roll Player is that it can be utilized as an accompaniment for role-playing. Add on that it’s pretty outstanding on its own, and you realize, this is a pretty first-rate board game.

I give it a: B+

Graham reviews Roll Player

If you’ve ever played Dungeons & Dragons, you know how much fun creating a character is. It’s all an amusing experience – from getting to choose your class and race, rolling some dice, allocating them to specific ability scores, and finishing it off with an exciting backstory. This idea was clearly the influence of Keith Matejka’s Roll Player.

Gold pieces that can be used to purchase items from the market.

I’ll be honest here, I didn’t do much research on Roll Player before I played it for the first time. So, I didn’t really know what it was about other than the fact that it was called “Roll Player”. Going into my first game, I had expectations of actually role-playing, but role-playing is not really required for this game. In fact, there really isn’t much character interaction at all in Roll Player, which is really my main criticism of it. During our game, I never felt like I was controlling a unique character that I was taking on this fantastic journey. There was no point to creating the characters other than matching goals that we had to meet. I feel any theme could have been put on top of this game and it wouldn’t have changed my experience at all. Another criticism I have of Roll Player is how it tries to balance the game for the person who chooses their character last (in a three player game). I mean, one extra gold piece doesn’t really matter all that much in the grand scheme of things, while your class choice shapes your entire strategy. In other words, as the last person, you are already at somewhat of a disadvantage. This is a minor criticism, but it still effects the game nonetheless.

It’s a pretty hefty bag of dice.

That being said, there are things I appreciate about this game. First, for a dice-rolling game, it seems as though luck isn’t a major factor (other than determining turn order in the beginning and your first attribute rolls). There are plenty of situations where rolling smaller numbers can help, and there even are cards that reward you for have low attribute scores. Another thing I like about Roll Player is the dice manipulation and their mechanics, which are very well thought-out and really require some logic and strategy to think through. The dice manipulations include swapping two dice, flipping a dice to its opposite side, raising or lowering a dice’s value, re-rolling a dice, moving your alignment cube, or reducing the cost of a market item. So as you can see, there are tons combinations and possibilities, and all of them can be useful depending on your situation or strategy.

Roll Player is a game that I had fun playing, but looking back on it, I’m not really sure what my overall feeling is on it. On one hand, it’s a unique game that got me thinking and strategizing. On the other hand, there was a disconnection between the theme and how the game played. Because of this uncertainty, I’m going to give Roll Player one thumb up and one thumb down. I would like to emphasize that “one thumb up and one thumb down” does not mean I disliked my experience or won’t play this game again. Roll Player is enjoyable, and you should definitely try it out for yourself! I just think it could use some changes or improvements to turn it into a great game.

The new expansion, Roll Player: Monsters & Minions, is currently on Kickstarter and scheduled for a release of February 2018. I’m excited to see how the expansion improves upon the current version.
