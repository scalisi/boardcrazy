Board Crazy Reviews Tiny Epic Galaxies

This week, Board Crazy reviews Tiny Epic Galaxies, a board game designed by Scott Almes and published by Gamelyn Games. In Tiny Epic Galaxies, players compete to become the most influential galactic empire! Is it truly as tiny and epic as the name implies? Keep reading to see what we think!

D reviews Tiny Epic Galaxies

(Author’s note: this review is meant to accompany our gameplay video and will not go in-depth on the game’s rules. If you’re interested in learning how the game is played, please watch the video. It’s not bad.)

Scott Almes’ Tiny Epic Galaxies is almost certainly the finest entry of Gamelyn’s Tiny Epic family of games, although that praise may not be as high as one might hope. This series has a concept that I find commendable: offering experiences similar to big box strategy games, but condensed down into a small, reasonable package. I love those big box games, but finding space to play them and then having to set up and clean up dozens (or even hundreds) of components naturally feels more like work than play. As such, I think the service these games offer is undeniably awesome for the bits right before and right after you play them. Unfortunately, I still don’t think they’ve nailed that all-important part that happens in between.

Aughmoore – A diplomacy based planet card. It’s worth 7 victory points and allows you to aquire culture for every ship landed in your galaxy.

Before I start to sound too negative, I should reiterate that Tiny Epic Galaxies is probably the high point of the series so far. It is a fun game that is capable of delivering on most of the game features promised on the box. For starters, it is legitimately easy to understand the rules and it shouldn’t take long at all to get started with first timers, assuming they have some experience with board games. The strategy involved, though, is surprisingly complex. There are several aspects of this game which you must always be mindful of, such as which dice to keep, which to re-roll, and then in which order to play them. Also, you can’t ignore your opponents and their own progress, as the ability to follow actions is one of the major keys to success. Additionally, the replayability is high, as there are a whole lot of planet cards included and a solid number of secret mission cards. The game also plays quite differently depending on how many players you have, and it even functions quite well as a single-player game.

The Hoarder Secret Mission Card

There are some problems, though. That bit about the game playing differently with different numbers of players is more of a flaw than anything else. I can recommend the game for one, three and four players, but it suffers a bit if you play with two or five. The game just doesn’t seem built for two-player duels, as the planet abilities and rules themselves generally seem to discourage competing over planets, leading to a rather stale experience. Games with five are mechanically solid, but I think the game is actually too complex to work with that number. The box claims the game has a 30-45 minute play time, and while these estimates are rarely accurate in my experience, a game with five players can easily go on for two hours, assuming everyone is trying to win. I certainly don’t mind long games, but I don’t think Tiny Epic Galaxies is designed to be enjoyable over that length of time. Three or four players seems to be the sweet spot, but even then the game has what I consider to be a couple of fundamental flaws holding it back.

The dice and all of the actions you can take.

First – and stop me if you’ve heard this one before – I am not keen on using dice to determine something as important as the actions you can perform on your turn. The game does provide some options in order to address this obvious concern, but there is always a limit to what you can do if the dice gods aren’t with you. Randomness is always a major factor in Tiny Epic Galaxies, which is something I will always rail against. The other major issue I have is something that is probably apparent if you’ve watched our recorded game, which is that the following mechanic is imbalanced. Since it allows you to follow your opponents’ actions, culture is the easily most valuable resource in a game where all of the other resources seem to have about equal value. That you can follow when an opponent earns culture of their own, and potentially increase your own culture almost constantly, makes it broken as far as I’m concerned. I realize that this option is available to everyone, but in many cases doing so will be in disservice to a player’s own, more important goals. I’d like to play a game where you can’t follow when an opponent earns culture or energy and see how that plays, because as it is I’m really bothered by it.

And I think I’m really bothered because this game has so much promise. Most of the time, a shorter game like this is what I’m looking for when I’m hanging out with friends, and I haven’t played too many alternatives which offer such a nice balance of strategy and simplicity (at least for groups of three or four people). It’s also really well made – it has good (if slightly small) components, which make good use of color. The planet designs and names add some nice flavor, while the rulebook is well-written and features plenty of pictures for clear examples. And the gameplay can be fun, at least as long as the dice are rolling in your favor. I think it’s a decent game that’s a couple of tweaks away from being a great game, but check it out if it looks like something up your alley in our video.

D’s Rating: Three Stars out of Five.

Will reviews Tiny Epic Galaxies

Tiny Epic Galaxies is one of those games where I bet that my colleagues will have a lot of the same points as I do. After all, the game is “tiny”, or in other words, there aren’t that many mechanics to deal with. This simplicity works both for the game and against it – almost to the point where I feel as though the designer could have made a few tweaks to make it even simpler and thus, better. Regardless, what we have here is a small game that packs a lot of punch, and that is, at the very least, commendable in its own right.

Ultimately, the merit of a game comes down to its gameplay, and as I already pointed out, it’s a bit of mixed bag here. Essentially, Tiny Epic Galaxies is a dice-rolling game, but instead of numbers on the sides of each die, you have symbols that relate to the actions the players can perform. There’s also a row of planet cards that present additional ways to earn special actions and most importantly, victory points. Most of the dice actions (movement, economy, etc.) are designed to aid players in moving around the planets and colonizing for points. For the most part, this portion of the gameplay is balanced and fair, or at least, it is as much as a dice-rolling game can be. Traversing the galaxy looking for colorful planets to colonize is fun and ignites the imagination of the players. Without a doubt, this is my favorite part of Tiny Epic Galaxies. Unfortunately, there’s a resource side of the game, and this is where it loses me.

The galactic empire’s game board. Each player get’s one to manage their resources, ships, and empire upgrades.

There are two resources in Tiny Epic Galaxies: culture and energy. They can both be acquired with dice rolling as long as you have a ship on a planet or galaxy with the corresponding symbol. And they each have their own purpose, but they are definitely not equal, and this is a huge problem. Let’s start with energy, which can be spent to re-roll dice. In actuality, energy is mainly used to upgrade your empire, thus earning you additional ships, dice, and victory points. Now, you might find yourself thinking, “But Will, that sounds awfully important”, and you’d be right. However, the culture resource can also be spent to do the exact same action, and it has additional power: culture allows players to follow (duplicate) another player’s actions. This all means one thing: the player who acquires the most culture the earliest wins the game. This is basically a definite in every game of Tiny Epic Galaxies.

Admittedly, the designer (Scott Almes) tried his hardest to balance the culture resource with a number of restrictions. For one, there’s no guarantee that culture based planets will be drawn in quantity at the beginning of a game. Two, there’s a limit of seven for both energy and culture, so you can’t collect endless amounts of either. And three, you can only gather culture from matching planets, whereas energy can be acquired from your own galaxy. Tack on the unpredictability of dice rolls and you would think there’s enough to balance it, but there just isn’t. I’m not sure what the solution is – maybe there needs to be a limit of five culture, or maybe players should be unable to follow when someone else is collecting culture. I’m not a game designer, so maybe I’m out of my depth here, but this is where I would have simplified things a bit. One thing I know for certain, the culture resource breaks the game and subsequently, my desire to play it.

In the end, this all means that one lucky player can run away with a game of Tiny Epic Galaxies on the first turn of a session. If you’re lucky, there’s a chance that there will be more energy based planets and some crappy dice rolling that will allow the game to develop evenly, but this hasn’t happened often with my group. Still, Tiny Epic Galaxies is a well-crafted (great build quality), small package that packs a good amount of punch, for what it is. Perhaps I should give it a break, considering it was never meant to compete with the big boys. All in all, it’s decent – what more can someone ask from a board game that can fit in a purse.

I give it a: B-

Graham reviews Tiny Epic Galaxies

Just from the name alone, you understand what designer Scott Almes was trying to achieve with Tiny Epic Galaxies – he wanted to give us a big game in a small package. I won’t lie, as much as I love the massive 4X games like Twilight Imperium, I think twice before playing them just because of the commitment that’s involved. That’s why I appreciate what Scott Almes was trying to accomplish, but let’s not pretend that Tiny Epic Galaxies will ever replace a game like Twilight Imperium. It doesn’t need to. Instead, let’s look at Tiny Epic Galaxies as it is, a lightweight dice-rolling/worker-placement game with some cool mechanics.

I enjoy Tiny Epic Galaxies, but it definitely has some issues. First and foremost, the culture mechanic, as awesome as it is, is totally broken. In this game, you are allowed to follow (or copy) the move of any of your opponent’s activated dice if you pay one culture. So if they activate a ship movement die, then you can pay one culture to move one of your ships wherever you want. There really is no point to try any other strategy other than collecting culture and following your opponent when you need to. The only opposing strategies I can think of are just doing the exact same thing and hoping you can do it better, or trying to take the culture cards as quickly as you can by colonizing those planets. Both strategies are a coin flip though. I found our playthroughs becoming more and more predictable, and this hurts the game’s replayibility. The same strategies get used over and over again, and each game starts feeling like the one before. I don’t want the Follow mechanic gone from the game, it just needs to be balanced better. Despite these issues, Tiny Epic Galaxies does have it’s redeeming qualities.

The pieces that come with each empire – Empire tracker, Culture tracker, Energy Tracker, and 4 ships.

My favorite part about Tiny Epic Galaxies is all of the cool mechanics you get to use. For example, even though it’s broken, it’s a lot of fun spending culture to use the follow mechanic or spending an energy to re-roll a die. It’s great that you can choose between just landing on a planet or trying to colonize it. The dice are fun to roll because all of the actions are fun to use. As I mentioned in my opening paragraph, Tiny Epic Galaxies will never replace the massive 4X games, but it does great job of incorporating a lot into such a tiny box. That is another thing I love about this game – it’s small, simple, and easy to explain, which makes it a great game to introduce to newbies. If you tried to introduce board games to someone with Twilight Imperium, they’d probably never talk to you again.

After looking at my own review here, it would seem that I almost have a more negative view on Tiny Epic Galaxies, but that’s not the case. Overall I like this game. Yes, it has some serious flaws, but it’s such a simple game and the mechanics are fun enough that I will continue to play it and recommend it. I am also excited to play Tiny Epic Galaxies: Beyond the Black, as well as Tiny Epic Quests, which is coming out in August.

I give Tiny Epic Galaxies one thumb up and one thumb down.
